From tree calculus to TC-Net

An interactive tour of two machines that compute the same answers very differently — a hash-consed reducer that shares and memoizes, and a materialized interaction net that deliberately does neither. Companion to tc-net.typ, RUST_IC_NET_DESIGN.md, and EVALUATOR.md.

disp's evaluator has a spec — five rewrite rules — and several machines that realize it. Two are worth contrasting head-to-head. The eager reducer (the checker's backend) interns every node and memoizes every application: it shares aggressively and equality is a pointer compare. The TC-Net reducer (the optimizer's substrate) does the opposite — it turns the whole computation into a graph of little machine-nodes and rewrites them locally, sharing nothing by default.

The surprise, which this page builds toward with real numbers: the net is not a speedup. On raw reduction it loses to the eager reducer by 20–550×. You materialize it anyway, on purpose, to buy two things hash-consing structurally cannot give you. Getting to why is the whole journey.

How to read this — what's real, what's schematic. Acts 0–4 run two actual reduction engines compiled into this page. The interaction counts, allocation counts, and δ copy-waves are computed, not narrated — and every time the net finishes, its normal form is checked against the eager reducer's (the same differential-oracle discipline the project uses to validate backends). Acts 5–6 are schematic diagrams and measurements lifted from the real Rust crate, because faithfully simulating 64-bit atomic exchanges and OS-thread work-stealing inside a browser tab would be theater, not truth. Where a number comes from the crate rather than this page, it says so.

Act 0 · the specificationTree calculus and its five rules

Tree calculus has one symbol, △, and binary application. Every value is a tree built from three shapes, distinguished only by how many arguments △ has so far received:

A leaf is bare △; a stem is △a (one child); a fork is △a b (two children). Leaves, stems, and forks are values — they sit still. Computation happens only when a fork receives a third argument.

At that moment one of five rules fires. The first two (K and S) are Turing-completeness; the last three (triage) are what lets a program branch on the shape of any value — including another program. That is self-reflection, and it falls out for free with no quotation or Gödel numbering.

△ △ y z          ⟶  y              — K   (return the first branch)
△ (△x) y z       ⟶  x z (y z)      — S   (the only rule that DUPLICATES)
△ (△w x) y △     ⟶  w              — triage: arg is a leaf
△ (△w x) y (△u)  ⟶  x u            — triage: arg is a stem
△ (△w x) y (△u v)⟶  y u v          — triage: arg is a fork

That is the entire semantics. Everything below is a machine that applies these rules — the rules never change. Here is "regular" tree-calculus evaluation: an application tree, with the next redex highlighted. Step it.

Step a reduction · plain rewriting

Internal nodes are applications (@); leaves are △. The highlighted subtree is the redex about to fire. Notice the S rule: it copies its argument into two places — keep an eye on that, it is the only source of duplication in the whole calculus and the entire story of Act 3.

Act 1 · machine oneThe eager reducer: sharing and memo

The reducer that the type-checker actually runs (src/core/tree.ts, and its Rust twin rust-eager) makes two moves that the plain rewriter above does not. Both are about not doing the same work twice.

Move 1 — hash-consing: identical trees are one node

Every node is interned in a table keyed by (tag, child-ids). Build stem(leaf) twice and you get back the same object the second time. A term is therefore not a tree but a DAG: repeated subterms are pointers to one shared node. Two consequences fall out immediately — memory is collapsed, and equality becomes O(1): two terms are equal iff they are the same pointer. That O(1) conversion check (tree_eq) is the single most load-bearing fact in the type checker.

Hash-consing a repeated subterm

Building fork(true, true) — both halves are the value true = △△.

Move 2 — memoization: applications are cached

Reduction is a function apply(f, x). Its result is cached in a table keyed by the pointer identities (f.id, x.id) — which only works because of hash-consing. Apply the same function to the same (interned) argument again and you pay nothing. This is cross-occurrence sharing of work, not just structure.

The apply-memo, live

A repeated apply(not, true) is a cache hit — zero reduction steps. A different argument misses and reduces. The cache key is pointer identity, so it depends entirely on Move 1.

The reframe that makes Act 2 land. The interaction-net vocabulary — apply, dispatch, duplicate, erase — is already here, hiding as the control flow of this apply function. Hash-consing is just an interaction net whose duplicators have collapsed into pointer-sharing and whose suspensions carry a memo cell. The next machine takes those hidden operations and makes every one of them a real, schedulable object in memory. That is the only difference — but it changes everything.

Act 2 · machine twoMaterialize it: the interaction net

An interaction net is a graph of agents. Each agent has one principal port and some auxiliary ports. When two agents meet principal-to-principal, that is an active pair (a redex), and a local rule rewrites just those two agents and their immediate wires. Nothing else in the graph is touched. TC-Net realizes tree calculus with ten agent kinds:

L leaf S stem F fork P suspended application f a    E demand a value A apply one arg T₁ dispatch lvl 1 T₂ dispatch lvl 2    δ duplicate ε erase

The four producers (L, S, F, P) are the data — trees and not-yet-evaluated applications. The six consumers are the verbs: they ask things of a producer's root. Evaluation is just consumers eating producers. A P node is the crucial one — it is a suspended f a with its own principal root, so it can be demanded, duplicated, or erased like any value. That "rooting" is the one repair that makes the whole thing work.

Two things to notice in the counters. First, erasure is real work: when the K and triage rules throw away a branch, an ε agent has to walk in and delete it, interaction by interaction. The eager reducer just stops pointing at it. Second, the totals are much larger than the eager reducer's handful of steps for the same answer — the net is paying, in allocations and interactions, for the privilege of having made every operation an explicit object. Hold that thought for Act 6.

Why no oracle? Encoding the λ-calculus into interaction nets famously needs bookkeeping nodes (brackets, croissants) and an "oracle" to decide when two duplicators annihilate vs commute — all to track variable scope through duplication. Tree calculus has no binders. The only duplication is the S-rule copying an argument, and the copies enter independent subcomputations that share no scope. So duplication here is always sound, and the hardest subsystem of every optimal-λ machine simply does not exist for us.

Act 3 · sharing, on purposeTwo duplicators: δˢ vs δⁿ

The eager reducer shares for free (that is hash-consing). A materialized net has to share by doing something — and there are two ways, which differ only in what happens when a duplicator meets a suspended computation P.

δˢ — structural copy

Meets a P, copies it as syntax — two suspensions, each demanded independently. The shared computation runs once per copy. Shares structure, never work.

δⁿ — demand-before-copy ("need")

Meets a P, demands it and parks itself on the result wire. The computation runs once; only finished constructors get copied. This is call-by-need rendered as a net.

Here is the spec's own worked case: a suspended not true that the S-rule duplicates into two strict positions. Flip the duplicator species and watch the cost move.

δˢ vs δⁿ on a shared suspension

With δˢ you can see the duplication of work in the copy-waves count: the suspension is copied as structure and then re-reduced on both sides. With δⁿ, demand-before-copy fires once, the work happens once, and only the resulting constructor is copied. Both produce the identical normal form (checked live) — they differ only in cost.

And the two species never need an oracle to coexist. A duplicator only ever faces a producer at its principal port, so δˢ and δⁿ can never meet each other principal-to-principal — no coherence rule between them is required. You can pick, per duplication site, which sharing you want: δⁿ where you want work shared (the S-rule), δˢ where a reflective program genuinely wants to copy an unevaluated program as data. This per-site choice is what a graded/quantitative type system can drive automatically — usage on the function type lowering to "which duplicator."

Act 4 · the payoffWhy materialize at all: parallelism & provenance

If the net is slower (Act 2) and you had to add work to recover the sharing the eager reducer got for free (Act 3), why build it? Two reasons, and both are things hash-consing structurally cannot do.

Reason 1 — parallelism

Recall strong confluence: distinct active pairs share no agents (each agent has exactly one principal port, so it is in at most one redex). Therefore every active pair in the bag can fire at the same time, and the order never changes the answer. The eager reducer's call stack is inherently sequential; the net's work-bag is a pool of independent jobs.

Below, N independent not true computations share one net. A round fires all currently-active pairs at once; rounds ≈ span (critical-path length) and interactions ≈ work (total). Slide the worker count and watch work spread across rounds.

Work vs span · parallel rounds

Each row is one synchronized round; colored cells are interactions firing in parallel. Independent work (this) spreads wide — span stays flat as you add jobs. A single sequential demand-chain (like Act 2's not true) would be one tall stripe: span ≈ work, no parallelism to extract. That is the honest caveat — the net hands you parallelism, but only where the work is actually independent.

Confluence makes the test suite a race detector. Because the normal form cannot depend on firing order, running the parallel net and the sequential reducer on the same term and comparing normal forms detects any data race for free: if they ever disagree, it is a concurrency bug, never a wrong verdict. The net you just ran was checked against the eager reducer — same discipline.

Reason 2 — provenance (the real reason)

This is the one that actually justifies the machine. The verified optimizer (disp's north star) searches for a better program and must attribute blame — "which candidate subterm caused this?" Hash-consing is fatal to that: it merges two distinct-but-equal subterms into one node. Wonderful for a checker; catastrophic for blame attribution, because once merged you can no longer tell the candidates apart. The materialized net keeps distinct-but-equal subnets distinct. Abandoning sharing is not a regression here — it is a prerequisite.

hash-consed · great for checking

candidate A ─┐
             ├─▶ one node   blame: ???
candidate B ─┘

Equal-by-evaluation ⇒ merged. O(1) conversion. No way to say which candidate mattered.

materialized · required for optimizing

candidate A ─▶ subnet A   blame: A
candidate B ─▶ subnet B   blame: B

Distinct subnets stay distinct. Reverse-mode "which input caused this output" becomes expressible.

A third use builds on the same property: superposition — packing nk candidate programs into one shared net and reducing them all at once. That is the optimizer's search engine, and it needs exactly this non-merging substrate. (Its soundness is still open research — δ must distribute over superposition — but the substrate it requires is precisely this one.)

Act 5 · under the floorboardsThe memory backend

So far "agent," "wire," and "the bag" have been abstract. Here is how the real Rust crate stores them — and why each choice is forced by the goals above. This section is a diagram, not a live sim: the operations below are 64-bit atomics on a flat arena, and faking that in JavaScript would only mislead.

The arena, not a heap of objects

  • A port is one tagged machine word — [tag : 4 bits | address : 28]. The tag is the agent kind, and it is carried in the referencing port ("kind-in-pointer"). So an active pair is two words that name both interacting kinds with zero memory loads: the rule dispatcher reads two 4-bit tags out of one 64-bit redex and jumps.
  • A node is a uniform fixed-width cell of auxiliary ports; the principal port is implicit — it is whichever slot references the node. No per-node header, no discriminant to load. Producers (≤2 aux) are one 2-slot cell; the wide dispatch agents T₁/T₂ are a contiguous block, read per-slot.
  • There is no global intern table. That table was hash-consing. Dropping it is what makes Act 4 possible — sharing is now expressed by wires and duplication by an explicit δ wave — and it is also the single biggest parallel-allocation win, because alloc no longer touches global shared state.

The linker: one atomic exchange, never a lock

The crown jewel, and it is agent-agnostic. To connect a wire you do one unconditional atomic_exchange (a swap) into a variable slot — never a compare-and-swap, never a lock:

fn link(neg, pos):
    if pos is not a Var:            // two principals → active pair
        bag.push(neg, pos)          // redex DETECTED at link time, never scanned
    else:
        prev = swap(slot[pos], neg)    // the ONE atomic op
        if prev == EMPTY: return    // we deposited our half; partner will find it
        else: relink(prev)          // raced: take partner's half, retry

It is race-free with no lock because every variable occurs exactly twice: the second linker to reach a slot atomically swaps out the first's deposited half and wins ownership. And we only ever need memory safety here — never result determinism — because strong confluence already guarantees the answer. The two nodes of a popped redex are exclusively owned by the firing worker (each had its single principal there), so it overwrites and reuses them in place; only links into far variable slots touch the atomic.

Garbage collection: the net is linear

A subtlety lazy interaction nets are infamous for: a parked δⁿ whose two copies are both discarded becomes unreachable (ε acts at principal ports; the parked duplicator hides on an auxiliary wire). The design's answer leans on structure:

  • The net is a DAG — recursion is a tree that unfolds, never a graph cycle — so reference counting is complete; no cycle collector is ever needed.
  • In the eager schedule it is even simpler: an interaction net is linear (each agent is consumed exactly once), so reclamation is just "free both agents of every active pair as it fires." Peak memory then tracks the live working set — in the real crate this took a fib(8) run from 7.6M nodes down to 64K (~119×). That is not an optimization; without it the net OOMs on anything real.

Act 6 · the verdictThe honest scorecard

Now the payoff of all the honesty. You have seen the net pay in interactions (Act 2), seen it claw back sharing only by adding machinery (Act 3), and seen what it buys (Act 4). Here is what the real crate measures when you race it against rust-eager on actual programs.

Reduction speed · native, best-of-3 (from npm run bench:ic-net)

Lower is faster. rust-eager hash-conses (O(1) conversion + cross-occurrence sharing); ic-net allocates per interaction and shares nothing. On the embarrassingly-parallel "wide" workload — ic-net's best case — rust-eager running sequentially still beats ic-net sequential ~4× (it shares the repeated work), and ic-net's parallel mode is worse still. Beating rust-eager on reduction was never the job.

The scheduler journey (a cautionary tale)

Making the parallel net actually go faster than its own single thread was not free either. The first parallel version was a 7× regression: per-interaction global atomics (a budget decrement, an interaction counter, an in-flight RMW — on every interaction) plus one shared work-queue meant cache-line ping-pong between cores serialized faster than parallelism recovered. The fix was to get coordination off the hot path entirely:

Measured on a 6-core Ryzen, 8 threads vs 1. Budget leasing → per-worker work-stealing deques (kill the shared queue) → per-worker bump-allocation regions. Net result: from a ~7× regression to a ~2.4× speedup — but it plateaus at ~6 cores because the materialized net is memory-bandwidth bound, and even at full tilt still trails rust-eager-native ~1.5–1.7×.

So when do you reach for which?

You want…UseBecause
Type-checking / conversioneager (rust-eager)O(1) equality is pointer identity; sharing & memo win sequential reduction outright. The net literally can't serve the checker's classify protocol.
Fast sequential reductioneagerHash-consing shares work across occurrences; nothing the net does is competitive here.
The verified optimizerTC-Net (ic-net)Needs per-candidate provenance — distinct-but-equal subnets must stay distinct. Hash-consing merges them; the net keeps them apart.
Massively independent workTC-NetStrong confluence ⇒ free, deterministic parallelism across the work-bag — where the work is genuinely independent.

The arc in one line: hash-consing collapses the interaction net into a fast sequential reducer by sharing structure and work; materializing the net un-collapses it, paying in speed to regain the two things that collapse destroyed — parallelism and provenance. Neither machine is "better." They sit at opposite ends of one design axis, and disp keeps both because the checker and the optimizer want opposite things.
